A Close-Reading & Comprehension Tool

TRUE GRIT

Charles Portis · 1968 · narrated by Mattie Ross of near Dardanelle in Yell County

"People do not give it credence that a fourteen-year-old girl could leave home and go off in the wintertime to avenge her father's blood but it did not seem so strange then, although I will say it did not happen every day." — the novel's opening sentence

Orientation

A short novel with a long reach. On its surface a frontier revenge tale; underneath, a study in voice, retributive justice, and the gap between how Mattie tells her story and what the story actually shows. Use this panel to fix the essentials before close reading.

The premise in one breath

In late 1870s Arkansas, Mattie Ross's father is murdered and robbed by his hired hand Tom Chaney, who flees into the Indian Territory. Fourteen, devout, and relentless, Mattie hires the dissolute one-eyed deputy U.S. marshal Rooster Cogburn — a man with "true grit" — and rides after Chaney herself, with a glory-seeking Texas Ranger, LaBoeuf, in tow.

The frame you must not forget

The entire book is narrated decades later by an elderly, one-armed, unmarried Mattie. Every judgment, scripture citation, and dry aside is the voice of the old woman, not the girl. The retrospective frame is the novel's central literary device — keep asking what the narrator emphasizes, omits, and refuses to feel.

Why it rewards a college reader

  • Unreliable-by-temperament narration. Mattie is factually scrupulous yet emotionally and morally self-certain. Irony lives in the distance between her plain reporting and the reader's response.
  • Calvinist substructure. Her Cumberland Presbyterian belief in predestination and "paying for everything" organizes the plot's economy of debts, contracts, and blood.
  • Deadpan comic register. Portis fuses violence with comedy through diction — legalistic, biblical, formal — that almost never breaks.
  • Revisionist Western. Written in 1968, it both honors and quietly dismantles frontier myth: heroism is shabby, drunk, transactional, and costs Mattie her arm.
A note on structure & accuracy: Portis's novel runs as a continuous first-person narrative without numbered or titled chapters. The "movements" in the Plot Walkthrough are this tool's pedagogical divisions of the action, not Portis's own headings. Plot points and quotations are drawn from the 1968 text; where a detail is summarized rather than quoted it is paraphrased faithfully.

Plot Walkthrough

Eight movements, in order. Open each for the events that matter and a close-reading prompt. Track three threads as you go: Mattie's vengeance, the money/contracts, and what the narration leaves unsaid.

1 · The murder & the journey to Fort Smith Inciting

Frank Ross is shot dead in Fort Smith by his hired hand Tom Chaney after a night of drinking and gambling; Chaney robs the body of two California gold pieces and a horse and flees across the Arkansas River into the Indian Territory, joining the outlaw Lucky Ned Pepper. Mattie travels to Fort Smith with the family hand Yarnell Poindexter to claim the body, then sends Yarnell home and stays to see justice done.

She witnesses a public triple hanging outside Judge Isaac Parker's court — two white men and a Native man (denied his last words) — establishing the law's grim machinery and the world's casual violence.

Close reading: Notice how flatly Mattie reports her father's corpse and the hangings. What does the absence of stated grief tell you about her, and about the narrating voice's discipline?

2 · The horse-trade with Colonel Stonehill Character

To fund the manhunt, Mattie out-negotiates the livestock trader Colonel G. Stonehill in the novel's celebrated bargaining duel. Invoking her family lawyer J. Noble Daggett as a weapon, she recovers money for the ponies her father bought, forces Stonehill to buy back the ponies, and reclaims her father's horse Judy — eventually acquiring her own pony, Little Blackie.

Close reading: This comic set-piece is pure plot economy and pure characterization. How does Mattie's mastery of contracts, threats, and law foreshadow how she'll treat revenge — as a debt to be collected?

3 · Hiring Rooster & the courtroom Rising

Mattie seeks the marshal with the most "grit" and is pointed to Reuben J. "Rooster" Cogburn — one-eyed, fat, a heavy drinker, a former Confederate guerrilla. She watches him testify at the Wharton trial, where defense lawyer Goudy cross-examines him and exposes his violent body count and loose regard for the law. She hires Rooster for the job; meanwhile the Texas Ranger LaBoeuf (pronounced "LaBeef") appears, also hunting Chaney — for murdering a Texas state senator and his dog — intending to take him back to Texas for the reward.

Close reading: The courtroom scene is Portis's sly way of vetting Rooster's "grit." Is it courage, brutality, or both? Track how the word "grit" shifts meaning across the book.

4 · Crossing into the Territory Conflict

Rooster and LaBoeuf try to leave Mattie behind and strike a deal to split the chase and the reward, sidelining her vengeance. Mattie refuses to be left; she fords the river on Little Blackie and forces her way into the expedition. The uneasy trio is set: a girl who wants Chaney hanged in Arkansas, a Ranger who wants him alive for Texas money, and a marshal in the middle.

Close reading: The river crossing is Mattie's literal and figurative refusal of her assigned place. Read it against every adult who tells her to go home.

5 · The dugout: Quincy & Moon Turn

At a remote dugout they capture two of Ned Pepper's young associates, Quincy and Moon. Under Rooster's interrogation Moon begins to talk; Quincy silences him by chopping at him with a bowie knife, and Rooster kills Quincy. The dying Moon reveals that Ned Pepper's gang is coming that night for fresh horses. The expedition lays an ambush.

Close reading: Violence here is sudden, intimate, and morally muddy. Note how the boys' deaths are reported without sentiment — and how the gang's arrival is set in motion by their betrayal.

6 · The ambush goes wrong Setback

The night ambush at the dugout misfires. Shots are exchanged, men fall, but Ned Pepper escapes with most of his gang — and Chaney is glimpsed among them. The trio has tipped its hand without taking its quarry, and must now pursue the gang deeper into the Territory toward a final confrontation.

Close reading: The hero's plan fails. How does Portis use anticlimax to deflate frontier-adventure expectations even as he keeps the suspense?

7 · Mattie meets Chaney · the meadow charge Climax

Alone at a creek to water the horses, Mattie comes face to face with Tom Chaney. She shoots him with her father's heavy Colt's dragoon revolver and wounds him, but the recoil knocks her down; Chaney seizes her and carries her to Ned Pepper. Ned, who keeps a rough code, leaves Mattie in Chaney's charge with orders not to harm her, then rides to face Rooster.

In the open meadow comes the book's iconic image: outnumbered, Rooster takes the reins in his teeth, a revolver in one hand and a rifle in the other, and charges the four mounted outlaws — answering Ned Pepper's taunt ("bold talk for a one-eyed fat man") with "Fill your hands, you son of a bitch!" Rooster's horse is shot from under him and pins him; as Ned Pepper closes to finish him, LaBoeuf kills Ned with an extraordinary long shot from his Sharps rifle.

Above the gang's position, Chaney turns on Mattie; LaBoeuf intervenes and is hurt, and Mattie finally shoots Chaney dead with the dragoon. The recoil hurls her backward — into a pit.

Close reading: Mattie gets her vengeance and is instantly punished by it. The recoil that throws her into the pit is almost too neat — read it as the novel's moral physics: "you must pay for everything."

8 · The pit, the ride, and the long aftermath Resolution & Epilogue

Mattie falls into a cave pit holding a corpse, bats, and a rattlesnake; she breaks her arm in the fall and is bitten by the snake. Rooster descends, kills the snake, and hauls her out. He then rides Little Blackie nearly to death to reach a doctor — when the horse can go no further he shoots it — and commandeers a wagon to carry her on. Despite the rescue, the venom and delay cost Mattie her arm, which is amputated below the elbow.

The novel leaps forward roughly twenty-five years. The narrator is now an elderly, one-armed, never-married woman who runs a bank near Dardanelle. She learns Rooster has been touring with a Wild West show alongside outlaws-turned-performers Cole Younger and Frank James; she travels to see him but arrives to find he has died three days earlier. She has his body moved to her family plot. LaBoeuf she never sees again. The book closes on her famous, flat valediction about the passage of time.

Close reading: The epilogue reframes everything. The cost of "grit" is a lonely, hardened life. What has Mattie gained, what has she lost, and does she — or the novel — admit the difference? End on the closing lines: "This ends my true account… Time just gets away from us."

Characters

Read each profile for who they are and what they want — the wants are what collide. The three principals want incompatible things from the same dead man.

Narrator & protagonist Mattie Ross
  • Fourteen during the action; an elderly spinster while narrating.
  • Devout Cumberland Presbyterian; literal-minded, shrewd, fearless, self-righteous.
  • Thinks in contracts, debts, and scripture; treats vengeance as a just account to be settled.
  • Wants: Chaney caught, tried, and hanged in Arkansas, for her father — not killed conveniently elsewhere.
Deputy U.S. Marshal Reuben J. "Rooster" Cogburn
  • One-eyed, overweight, middle-aged, frequently drunk; rode with Confederate guerrillas in the war.
  • Ruthless and quick to kill, yet bound by a rough personal code; grows protective of Mattie.
  • Lives above Chen Lee's grocery; keeps a cat named General Sterling Price.
  • Wants: at first the fee, then — increasingly — to see Mattie through alive. He is "grit" embodied: courage indistinguishable from brutality.
Texas Ranger LaBoeuf ("LaBeef")
  • Vain, talkative, proud of the Ranger badge and his spurs; a genuinely fine marksman with a Sharps rifle.
  • Clashes with both Mattie and Rooster, but proves brave when it counts (the long shot that saves Rooster).
  • Wants: Chaney taken alive to Texas for the reward on a murdered state senator — a motive directly at odds with Mattie's.
Antagonist Tom Chaney
  • Frank Ross's hired hand and murderer; marked by a powder-burn black spot on his cheek.
  • Cowardly and self-pitying; blames everyone but himself; known in Texas by another name (Theron Chelmsford).
  • Wants: to survive and evade consequences — a small, mean man, deliberately unworthy of the violence spent on him.
Outlaw leader Lucky Ned Pepper
  • Leader of the gang Chaney joins; wounded in the lip (his speech is affected).
  • Dangerous but honors a code — he spares Mattie and orders Chaney not to harm her.
  • Delivers the taunt "bold talk for a one-eyed fat man" before Rooster's charge.
Supporting The Fort Smith world
  • Colonel G. Stonehill — livestock trader Mattie outmaneuvers.
  • J. Noble Daggett — Mattie's family lawyer, invoked as a recurring threat.
  • Yarnell Poindexter — the Ross family's hired man, sent home from Fort Smith.
  • Quincy & Moon — young outlaws at the dugout.
  • Judge Isaac Parker — the historical "Hanging Judge" presiding over Fort Smith.

The triangle of motives (memorize this)

Mattie → ChaneyJustice as a paid debt: she wants him hanged in Arkansas for her father. Personal, legal, and religious all at once.
LaBoeuf → ChaneyProfit: he wants him alive to Texas for a senator's-murder reward. This pits him against Mattie's whole purpose.
Rooster → MattieBegins transactional (the fee), becomes paternal. Rooster is the hinge between the girl's vengeance and the Ranger's bounty.
Ned Pepper → ChaneyChaney is a liability inside the gang; Ned's code (sparing Mattie) ironically makes him more honorable than the man he shelters.
Mattie ↔ adult worldEveryone tells her to go home; the engine of the plot is her refusal.

Themes & Annotated Quotes

Four load-bearing ideas, each anchored to language. For analysis essays, pair a theme with its quote and the narrative frame.

1 · Retributive justice & the economy of debt

Mattie's Calvinist worldview turns the plot into a ledger: crimes are debts, vengeance is collection, and grace is the only thing unpriced. The horse-trade, the marshal's fee, and the blood-debt are the same logic in different currencies.

"You must pay for everything in this world one way and another. There is nothing free except the Grace of God." — Mattie, narrating The thesis sentence of her morality. Read it forward into the snakebite and the lost arm: she, too, pays.

2 · "Grit" — courage, or its harder cousins

The title word is interrogated, not celebrated. Rooster's grit is bravery, stubbornness, and near-savagery at once; Mattie's is faith fused with will. The courtroom scene and the meadow charge ask whether the virtue can be separated from its violence.

"Fill your hands, you son of a bitch!" — Rooster Cogburn, charging Ned Pepper's gang The mythic image of the book. Note that Portis immediately undercuts heroism: the horse is killed, Rooster is pinned, and a Texan with a rifle actually decides the fight.

3 · The retrospective frame & the withheld self

The old narrator never grants herself a moment of stated grief, doubt, or tenderness. Meaning gathers in what she will not say. The same flat diction reports a hanging, a bargain, and her father's body.

"People do not give it credence that a fourteen-year-old girl could leave home and go off in the wintertime to avenge her father's blood…" — opening sentence The defensiveness ("do not give it credence") signals a narrator arguing with an imagined doubter across decades — the whole book is her brief in her own defense.

4 · The cost of vengeance & the revisionist West

Written in 1968, the novel honors the adventure form while charging it a real price. Mattie wins — and ends alone, one-armed, unmarried, wealthy, and unbending. The frontier produces no romance, no community, only a closed account and an old woman's loneliness.

"Time just gets away from us." — Mattie, the book's final line The flattest possible elegy. After a life organized around collecting a debt, the one thing she cannot bargain with is time. Let students argue whether this is wisdom, denial, or both.

Essay prompts (advanced)

  • Mattie claims to give a "true account." Build a case that the novel's irony lives in the distance between her accuracy and her self-knowledge.
  • Trace the word "grit" through Rooster, Mattie, LaBoeuf, and Ned Pepper. Is it a virtue, a temperament, or a justification?
  • Read the snakebite-and-amputation as the literalization of "you must pay for everything." What is Mattie paying, and to whom?
  • How does the 1968 publication date shape the novel as a response to — not just an example of — the Western?
  • Argue that Rooster's drunken, transactional heroism is a deliberate demolition of the marshal archetype.

Vocabulary & Historical Context

Portis's authority comes from period-precise diction. Knowing these terms is half of comprehension; the other half is hearing the formality they create in a teenager's mouth.

Period terms & objects

TermMeaning & significance
Colt's dragoonThe heavy cap-and-ball revolver Mattie carries as her father's gun. Too big for her — its recoil repeatedly knocks her down, and ultimately throws her into the pit. A literal weight of inheritance and vengeance.
Sharps (carbine/rifle)LaBoeuf's accurate long-range rifle. The instrument of his redemptive long shot that kills Ned Pepper and saves Rooster — competence, not bravado, wins the fight.
Deputy U.S. marshalFederal lawman with jurisdiction in the Indian Territory (Rooster). Distinct from a Texas Ranger (LaBoeuf), a state officer — the jurisdictional gap drives their rivalry over Chaney.
Indian / Choctaw NationThe Indian Territory (now Oklahoma), outside ordinary state law, where outlaws fled. The chase's lawless ground.
California gold piecesThe two coins Chaney robs from Frank Ross's body — the petty sum that the whole blood-quest answers, underscoring how small the crime's profit is.
Coverlet · coal oil · ague · draughtA bedspread; kerosene (for lamps); malarial fever/chills; a drink or dose. Everyday 1870s texture that grounds the narration.

People, places & allusions

ReferenceWhy it matters
Judge Isaac ParkerThe historical "Hanging Judge" of the Fort Smith federal court — real authority lending the novel documentary weight.
Quantrill's guerrillasThe Confederate irregulars Rooster rode with in the Civil War, explaining his comfort with extralegal violence.
Cole Younger & Frank JamesReal outlaws who later toured Wild West shows — Portis places Rooster among them in the epilogue, fusing history and myth and marking how the violent West curdled into entertainment.
Cumberland Presbyterian / predestinationMattie's denomination and theology. Her belief that events are foreordained and debts must be paid is the spine of her character and the book's moral logic.
Dardanelle, Yell CountyMattie's Arkansas home, repeated almost as an identity badge. The repetition is part of her self-authenticating voice.
"True grit"Said of Rooster when Mattie asks who has the most of it. The title turns a colloquialism into the novel's central question.
Voice exercise: Read any Mattie sentence aloud and mark the formal/legal/biblical words a real 14-year-old would not use. That gap — childlike position, lawyerly diction — is Portis's style. Comprehension of True Grit is mostly comprehension of that voice.
